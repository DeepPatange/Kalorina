The Art of Site Selection — Why the Land Comes First.

Before a single wall is drawn, we spend months on the plot. Walking it in different light, across different seasons. Watching how rain moves, where the mango trees drop shade at three o'clock, where the wind comes in.

AuthorSheel Kumar
PublishedApril 2026
Reading time8 min
CategoryCraft · Philosophy

The first time I walked the land at Karjat, it was four o'clock on a Tuesday in March. The heat was just beginning to soften. I didn't carry a sketchpad. I didn't carry a measuring tape. I carried a bottle of water, a notebook, and a kind of question I have learned to ask the land before it answers: what do you already know that we don't?

Eight weeks later, I had walked that plot forty-three times. In monsoon rain that turned the red earth to something closer to clay than ground. At six in the morning when a breeze I didn't know came across the lake. On a hot May afternoon when even the crickets stopped. Only then did we draw a line.

Chapter 01 · The First WalkWhy the land comes first.

In most residential development, the order goes roughly like this: buy the plot, appoint an architect, produce a design, build. The site is a surface you put architecture onto. It is a backdrop, not a collaborator. I understand the commercial logic of that sequence. I also believe it produces buildings that feel, a decade later, as if they could have been anywhere.

At Karolina we reverse it. The land is not a surface. It is the first creative director of the residence. The house's orientation, its footprint, its roofline, the trees we will keep and the ones we will carefully ask to move — all of this is decided with the plot, not for it.

We weren't designing a house on this land. We were allowing the land to decide where a house might sit.
Abhishek Verma, Architectural Partner

This sounds romantic. It is also practical. A villa oriented to catch the May evening breeze will run its air-conditioning three months less of the year. A pool aligned on the horizon line of the sea beyond will feel twice as long as its dimension. A kitchen window placed to watch the mango tree will, in its owner's eventual seventieth summer, still earn its keep. The land rewards attention.

Chapter 02 · Four SeasonsWhat a plot teaches over time.

We try to see every plot across every season before we break ground. In Goa, that means monsoon. In Karjat, it means the dry months when the lake's edge retreats. In Marna, it means the hilltop in December fog.

Some things the land only reveals slowly. Where the runoff pools after the first heavy rain tells you more about drainage than any soil report. Where the swallows come in September tells you something about the prevailing evening wind. The shade map at 3:30 pm in April is nothing like the one at 3:30 pm in July. If you've only seen the plot once, in good weather, you have not yet met it.

Chapter 03 · ListeningThe tools of attention.

We carry almost nothing on a site walk. A sun-path diagram — we want to know exactly where the 4 o'clock October sun falls. A wind-rose for the site's district, so we know which direction the monsoon will come from and which walls will take the rain. A contour map at half-metre precision, because even a seventy-centimetre slope changes where the pool makes sense.

But more than any tool, we carry patience. The instinct, when you are paying for a plot, is to start. To brief the architect, to publish renders, to announce the project. We have learned to resist that instinct. A premature brief narrows the land into a program before the land has had a chance to speak.

Small things we notice, almost always.

One: which of the existing trees are genuinely old. A mango of forty years is a different kind of decision than a coconut of six. We will move earth to save the forty-year tree. We will rarely move earth to save the six-year one.

Two: what neighbours the plot — not just the physical neighbours, but the social ones. Is there a church nearby whose bells are part of the Sunday experience? A rice mill whose sound is part of the morning? These are not things to be fought. They are part of the brief.

Three: where the locals walk. Older villages have footpaths that have existed longer than any deed. They tell you which boundary is a property line and which is a lived-in relationship between neighbours. The difference matters enormously to the feel of a finished home.

The land speaks first. Our role is to listen carefully — and then to build with the quiet confidence of something that has always belonged there.
The Karolina Philosophy

Chapter 04 · The Ethical SiteNot every plot becomes a villa.

We say no often. More often than I expected when we started. Sometimes the land is beautiful but the access road would require cutting through a neighbour's orchard. Sometimes it's a perfect footprint but the water table is not ours to assume. Sometimes the owners who came before us planted something that outlives any residence we might build, and the right answer is to walk away.

Saying no is, quietly, a core part of the Karolina method. Our portfolio is smaller than it could have been. We believe that is a feature, not a cost. Every villa we decline makes the villas we build more considered.

So when a client asks us, how long until we can start? — the honest answer is: we've already started, the day we saw the land. The sketching is a later stage in a much longer act of reading. What the client sees as patience is, from our end, the most important part of the work. The walls will come. The plot is already here. We just have to deserve it.

— Sheel Kumar, Founder. Written from the studio, Mumbai · April 2026.
